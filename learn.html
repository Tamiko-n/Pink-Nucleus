Catching it early changes everything

Breast cancer is one of the most common cancers, but it is also one of the most treatable when found early. Knowing what to look for and getting screened on schedule are the simplest, most powerful steps anyone can take.

What breast cancer is

Breast cancer begins when cells in the breast start to grow and divide in an uncontrolled way, forming a lump or mass. A benign mass is not cancer and does not spread; a malignant mass is cancerous and can grow into nearby tissue or spread elsewhere. Telling the two apart early is exactly what screening and tests like a fine–needle aspiration are designed to do, and it is the core of what this project models.

Why early detection matters

When breast cancer is found while it is still small and confined to the breast, treatment is far more likely to succeed and is often less aggressive. Survival rates are dramatically higher for localized disease than for cancer that has already spread. Early detection does not prevent cancer, but it buys the most important thing in cancer care: time and options.

How it is found early

Know your body

Being familiar with how your breasts normally look and feel helps you notice changes early and raise them with a doctor.

Screening mammogram

An X-ray that can spot tumours before they can be felt. Current US guidance advises average-risk women to screen every two years from age 40 to 74.

Clinical exam & tests

A clinician may add an exam, ultrasound, or a biopsy such as a fine-needle aspiration to examine the cells directly.

Changes worth checking with a doctor

A new lump or thickening in the breast or underarm
A change in the size or shape of the breast
Dimpling, puckering, or skin that looks like orange peel
Nipple turning inward, or unusual discharge
Redness, scaling, or flaking around the nipple
Persistent pain in one spot that does not go away

Most changes turn out not to be cancer, but only a clinician can tell. Checking early is always the right call.
